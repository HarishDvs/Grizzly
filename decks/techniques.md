# The Technique Deck — Named Structural Moves

Structural techniques with nameable mechanisms, distilled from webnovel/serial
craft discussion (Royal Road author forums, cliffhanger taxonomies by September
C. Fawkes and others, Wildbow's serial-writing essays) and reader analysis of
long-running serials (Lord of the Mysteries, Shadow Slave, Reverend Insanity,
The Legendary Mechanic, Worm). Every entry: mechanism (cause → reader effect),
when to use, example, failure mode.

Used by grizzly-plan (propose 1-2 fitting moves, never a menu) and grizzly-review
(name the move a flat stretch is missing).

---

## Chapter endings and hooks

### Pre-point cliffhanger (cut before the turn)
**Mechanism:** end one beat before the climactic moment resolves; the reader's mind
simulates the outcome and must return to check its guess.
**Use when:** genuine arc peaks, two or three times per arc at most.
**Example:** the detective opens their mouth to name the killer; chapter ends.
**Failure mode:** cutting a split second before every killing blow teaches readers
the trick; manipulation fatigue is the most-repeated hook complaint on author forums.

### Climactic mid-turn cut (revelation given, action withheld)
**Mechanism:** when a turning point splits into realization and act, deliver the
realization and cut before the act; partial resolution plus a guaranteed consequence.
**Use when:** the turn genuinely has two linked beats.
**Failure mode:** forcing a split where the moment is one beat reads as an arbitrary
scene break.

### Post-point cliffhanger (aftermath question)
**Mechanism:** deliver the twist in full, cut before anyone reacts or explains;
converts "what happens" into "why" and "what now."
**Use when:** the reveal's implications are richer than the reveal.
**Example:** the villain unmasked as a known character; end before the motive.
**Failure mode:** if the aftermath has no real weight, the chapter ends flat after
its own peak.

### Post-hook (new disruption after resolution)
**Mechanism:** resolve the arc, give the exhale, then destabilize in the final
lines; closure and a new open loop coexist, denying the reader a safe exit point.
**Use when:** arc endings.
**Failure mode:** stacked on every arc, victories feel worthless. Cannot open a
story (no investment yet).

### Decision cliffhanger
**Mechanism:** end at the moment of a costly choice and withhold it; tension comes
from psychology the reader has modeled, not external danger.
**Use when:** character-driven chapters where both options genuinely cost.
**Failure mode:** if one option is obviously right, withholding reads as stalling.

### Question hook (the sustainable default)
**Mechanism:** end on a small unresolved question, an odd detail, a hint; pull
without spending adrenaline.
**Use when:** the majority of chapters. Royal Road consensus: every chapter needs a
hook, not a cliffhanger.
**Failure mode:** questions too trivial to matter read correctly as noise.

### Hook-intensity rotation
**Mechanism:** vary ending types (danger, revelation, decision, lull, question)
across any 5-10 chapter span so no pattern becomes predictable.
**Use when:** as a planning-level check, always.
**Example:** Wildbow notes dense schedules force "softer cliffhangers" to avoid
fragmenting the story.
**Failure mode:** random variety with no escalation logic dissipates arc momentum.

### Structural cut-away (the act-out)
**Mechanism:** at a peak, jump to another POV or storyline; the suspended thread
generates tension underneath chapters nominally about something else.
**Use when:** multi-thread arcs; to lend urgency to necessary slower material.
**Example:** Worm's interludes run side-character POVs while the main plot hangs
at a crisis.
**Failure mode:** cutting from the thread readers care about to one they don't is
a toll, not a tease ("skipped the interlude").

## Tension engines

### The false lull (false peace)
**Mechanism:** a beat or chapter of safety, warmth, or apparent control; genre-aware
readers generate their own dread before anything goes wrong.
**Use when:** directly before disasters; as the breath inside action chapters.
**Example:** Worm repeatedly grants small wins immediately before escalation.
**Failure mode:** never paid off, readers stop trusting calm and skim it; always
paid off identically, calm becomes a countdown timer with no surprise.

### Perspective fracture (omissions and contradictions)
**Mechanism:** the same event shown from different POVs with deliberate gaps or
small contradictions between accounts; the reader assembles the truth by
triangulation and feels smarter than any single narrator.
**Use when:** mysteries about WHO or WHY; villains whose nature is the secret;
re-illuminating an early arc late.
**Example:** Lord of the Mysteries re-frames early events repeatedly as the
protagonist's knowledge grows; classic form is Rashomon.
**Failure mode:** contradictions readers can't detect are continuity errors, not
technique; the gap must be findable.

### No-safety-net stakes (the early kill)
**Mechanism:** establish through actual on-page losses, early, that the world's
logic does not protect the likeable; every later threat then carries real
probability weight.
**Use when:** early in the work, and refreshed at least once per arc.
**Example:** Reverend Insanity's world rules make no character morally insured.
**Failure mode:** gratuitous loss with no narrative consequence trains readers to
disinvest from everyone, which kills tension instead of raising it.

### Information-asymmetry tension (scheme versus scheme)
**Mechanism:** conflicts decided by who knows what, not raw power; the reader
tracks knowledge states and tenses whenever someone acts on wrong information.
**Use when:** any conflict a power-level comparison would predetermine.
**Example:** The Legendary Mechanic runs sustained dramatic irony, the protagonist
exploiting players who don't know he knows their nature.
**Failure mode:** schemes explained at full length after the fact convert suspense
into homework.

### Identity-pressure dread (the self as the stake)
**Mechanism:** the protagonist's own power threatens their sanity or identity, so
dread persists in quiet chapters because the danger travels inside the character.
**Use when:** external threats alone can't sustain tension between arcs.
**Example:** Lord of the Mysteries' acting method: fail to inhabit the persona and
go mad.
**Failure mode:** if the protagonist never visibly slips, the pressure becomes
lore instead of tension.

### Hostile environment as standing antagonist
**Mechanism:** the setting itself is lethal and sensorily rendered, so travel,
rest, and exploration carry baseline tension between plot beats.
**Use when:** between major antagonist arcs; to give quiet chapters teeth.
**Example:** Shadow Slave's locations are credited by readers with sustaining
engagement across hundreds of chapters.
**Failure mode:** an environment that menaces but never collects becomes wallpaper.

### Long-fuse foreshadowing with mundane camouflage
**Mechanism:** plant load-bearing facts disguised as boring texture long before
payoff; twists land as inevitable, rereads get rewarded.
**Use when:** continuously, from chapter one, for any planned reveal.
**Example:** Lord of the Mysteries plants Volume 1 details that pay off in Volume 6.
**Failure mode:** many fuses with no intermediate payoffs reads as chapters of
fruitless investigation (the standard pacing complaint against slow stretches).

## Progression and power

### Cost-bearing power (every gain carries dread)
**Mechanism:** every advancement or artifact carries a mandatory drawback, so a
power-up simultaneously raises capability and plants a threat the reader waits on.
**Use when:** progression systems and item economies in long serials.
**Example:** Lord of the Mysteries' cursed artifacts and corruption rules.
**Failure mode:** costs stated but never collected become decorative; readers stop
fearing them.

### Sparse, earned progression
**Mechanism:** ration power-ups so each is rare and consequential; the threat
ceiling stays above the protagonist and advancement chapters are events.
**Use when:** any progression serial past its opening arcs.
**Example:** Shadow Slave's restraint is credited with keeping advancement
impactful; Lord of the Mysteries gates progression behind money and obligation.
**Failure mode:** starving the genre's core promise entirely shows up as retention
cliffs on slow-burn serials.

### Preparation-visible plot armor
**Mechanism:** show caution, contingencies, and schemes on the page before the
crisis; survival reads as earned, and the fear of the unprepared moment stays live.
**Use when:** competent protagonists whose deaths the reader doesn't believe in.
**Example:** The Legendary Mechanic's protagonist rarely fights unprepared, which
readers cite as why his wins never feel like authorial rescue.
**Failure mode:** preparation that covers everything turns chapters into procedure.

### Break-and-recover cycle
**Mechanism:** let the protagonist genuinely break, then climb back; investment
attaches to endurance and the next blow is feared rather than discounted.
**Use when:** long arcs where victory alone would flatten the emotional line.
**Example:** Shadow Slave's hardest arcs are the ones readers cite for this.
**Failure mode:** breaks that reset cleanly by the next chapter read as melodrama
with no cost.

## Pacing and retention

### The quiet chapter with a live wire
**Mechanism:** follow action with a reflective, relational chapter so stakes
consolidate, but keep one unresolved thread visibly humming through it (an
unanswered letter, an absent character, a deadline) so pull never hits zero.
**Use when:** after every major action sequence.
**Failure mode:** two fully tensionless chapters in a row; in serial time that is
days with no pull, the classic point where follower counts dip.

### Compression over restatement in action
**Mechanism:** cut explanation of what the reader already saw; let combat imply
its own logic so momentum carries the tension.
**Use when:** all action sequences and post-deduction beats.
**Example:** readers fault explanation dumps after deductions, and report skimming
fights that move at one second per five paragraphs; both are named retention leaks.
**Failure mode:** overcorrecting into confusion where nobody can reconstruct who
did what.

### Consistent cadence with a buffer (serial logistics)
**Mechanism:** release on a predictable schedule backed by a pre-written backlog;
reading becomes a habit loop and missed updates never cascade.
**Use when:** from launch. Wildbow recommends a 12-16 chapter bank before
publishing, and warns monthly gaps leave readers a month to forget you exist.
**Failure mode:** chasing daily frequency at the cost of quality or forced soft
cliffhangers; fragments the story and burns out the author.
