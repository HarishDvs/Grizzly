# The Delivery Pass — Does It Land?

The blacklist removes tells. It cannot tell you whether the prose is any good. A
scene can be free of every em dash, filler line, and negation and still be dead on
the page: short true sentences, each one closed and sitting, no peak, nothing that
lands. That flatness is its own failure mode, and it is the exact one that
over-correcting on tells produces. When the only standard is "doesn't look like AI,"
the safest move is to flatten everything, and the result is competent reportage.
Clean is the floor, not the goal. Spare is not the same as empty.

Run this pass every time you draft or rewrite, after the blacklist pass. The
question is no longer "does this look like AI." It is "does this land."

---

## The benchmark method

`NOVEL.md` names the author's benchmark titles: published works whose amplitude the
author wants to be held against. Before showing a beat, hold it next to a scene from
a benchmark that does the same job (a reveal, a negotiation, a death, a quiet dread
beat, a character stepping into power). Do not copy diction. Do not borrow content.
Read for amplitude and technique:

- Do they build to a single hard landing where your draft keeps every sentence at
  the same level?
- Do they withhold and compress where your draft explains and resolves?
- Do they trust one exact image where your draft hedges with three soft ones?
- Is their dialogue loaded with what is not said, where your draft says the thing
  outright?

If your scene is flatter than the benchmark, it is not finished, even if it is
spotless.

---

## The six checks

1. **One landing per scene.** Name the single line, image, or beat the whole scene
   exists to deliver. If you cannot point to it, the scene has no peak and will
   read flat. Build one, or accept that the scene is only information.
2. **The landing is delivered, not summarized.** "He understood what command would
   cost him" is a summary of a landing. The landing arrives only through a concrete,
   bodily, or dramatic moment the reader lives through. If the most important beat
   is told in the flattest sentence in the scene, it is buried.
3. **Pressure under the surface.** What is each character NOT saying? Dialogue lands
   on the gap between the words and the want. On-the-nose dialogue, where people say
   exactly what they mean, is the talky-scene version of emotion-labeling.
4. **Momentum.** Each beat should open a question or pull toward the next. Flat
   prose closes every sentence completely and sits. Leave something unresolved that
   drags the eye forward.
5. **One exact, strange, world-specific detail.** A single precise detail (a
   survival habit, a ritual's cost, a thing a character does without thinking)
   carries more than a paragraph of mood. Reach for the one that could only exist
   in this story's world.
6. **Amplitude.** If every sentence is short, declarative, and fully closed, that IS
   the failure, even at zero tells. Vary the level. Let the scene run quiet and then
   land hard in one place.

---

## The reconciliation

Two failure modes pull in opposite directions:

- **Purple drift** (the blacklist guards against this): decorated, telling,
  machine-flavored prose.
- **Flat drift** (this pass guards against this): tell-free prose that does nothing.

The plain true line is the default texture, but every scene still needs the one
place it lands. If you catch yourself flattening a beat to stay safe, you have only
traded one failure for the other. The target is the loaded plain line, never the
empty one.

When you are unsure whether a beat lands, say so to the author and show the
benchmark comparison, rather than handing over a clean draft that does nothing.

---

## Three additive craft principles

**Sensory grounding.** When a scene leans cinematic (wide shots, camera moves,
visual-only description), pull it back into the body. Favor the senses a person
living in the scene would actually register: texture, smell, fatigue, pressure,
weight, temperature, breath, discomfort. A character notices the cold coming up
through the stone before they notice the architecture.

**Rhythm variation.** The default AI cadence is medium sentence, short sentence,
dramatic fragment, repeated. Break it. Vary sentence length, paragraph length,
dialogue pacing, and narrative density. Predictable rhythm reads as machine output
even when every individual line is clean.

**Compression.** Cut what doesn't earn its place: redundant description, an emotion
named twice, an observation repeated, an overexplained thought, decorative
adjectives. Keep only what reveals character, builds atmosphere, advances plot, or
tightens tension. When in doubt, the shorter version is usually truer.
