# Genre Pack — Webnovel / Serial Format

Data, not code. Applies when NOVEL.md declares format: serial. Combine with a
genre pack (this one governs FORMAT physics, not content). Never overrides
VOICE.md or STANDARDS.md.

## The format contract

A serial reader decides whether to return after EVERY chapter. Traditional novel
structure buys patience with a purchase; a serial earns it per installment. Two
consequences: every chapter needs its own reason to exist, and every chapter end
needs a pull.

## Chapter physics

- **The end-pull is mandatory.** Not always a cliffhanger; a pull. Taxonomy:
  - *Cliff* (danger unresolved): strongest, burns trust if every chapter uses it.
  - *Reveal* (new information reframes what the reader knew): the workhorse.
  - *Decision* (a character commits to something costly): durable, character-driven.
  - *Arrival* (a person/force enters at the worst moment): cheap to plant, strong.
  - *Question* (the chapter's events make a new question unavoidable): quietest,
    fine for breathers.
- **Open mid-scene where possible.** Serial openings carry returning readers over
  the release gap; a cold scenic open after a cliff wastes the cliff's energy.
- **One chapter, one job.** A serial chapter that half-does two things reads as
  filler twice.

## Cadence norms

- Vary the pull type; three cliffs in a row teaches readers to distrust the cliff.
- Arc length: long enough to matter, short enough that a binge reader feels arcs
  turning. Mark arc boundaries clearly (payoff chapter + breath chapter).
- **The breather rule:** quiet chapters are allowed and needed, but a breather
  still moves at least one open thread and still ends on a pull (usually
  *question* grade).
- Payoff cadence beats setup cadence: never run consecutive chapters that only
  plant. Readers feel debt accumulating even when they can't name it.

## Memory load (serial readers forget)

- Re-anchor names and stakes lightly on return: one identifying gesture or detail
  per returning character, folded into action, never a recap paragraph.
- The release gap means a planted thread needs a touch every so often to stay
  alive in reader memory; the codex's open-threads list is the tool for this.

## Failure modes (review flags)

- **Dead-end chapter:** no pull at the end; the single biggest retention leak.
- **Cliff fatigue:** identical pull type chapter after chapter.
- **Recap drag:** paragraphs re-explaining what happened, instead of one-line
  re-anchors in scene.
- **Setup stacking:** multiple consecutive chapters that open threads and pay none.
- **Binge seams:** chapters that work on release day but read as padding
  back-to-back. Review both ways when chapters are collected.
