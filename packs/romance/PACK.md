# Genre Pack — Romance

Data, not code. Applies when NOVEL.md declares the genre. Never overrides
VOICE.md or STANDARDS.md.

## The reader promise

An emotionally satisfying, optimistic ending for the central relationship (HEA:
happily ever after, or HFN: happy for now). This is the genre's defining contract;
a romance that breaks it is shelved as something else by readers, regardless of
label. Secondary promise: the relationship IS the plot. External events exist to
pressure, reveal, and change the pairing.

## Structural beats (the reader expects these; order can flex)

1. **The meet:** establishes the central tension between the leads in their first
   shared scene, not just their proximity.
2. **The reason they can't:** the obstacle, internal (wounds, beliefs) or external
   (rivalry, duty, secret). Internal obstacles age better; external-only obstacles
   collapse the moment circumstances change.
3. **Forced proximity / rising intimacy:** repeated contact the characters cannot
   avoid, each round costing more honesty.
4. **The midpoint shift:** the relationship changes state (admission, first kiss,
   alliance) and the old obstacle stops being enough; a deeper one surfaces.
5. **The dark moment:** the relationship appears genuinely lost, and the cause
   should trace to the leads' established wounds, not to a removable misunderstanding.
6. **The repair:** one or both leads change something real about themselves to
   close the gap. The gesture proves the change; the change is the point.

## POV conventions

- Dual POV is the genre default; it lets the reader hold both interiorities and
  feel the gap between what each lead believes. Single POV trades dramatic irony
  for mystery about the love interest; both work, choose deliberately.
- Whatever the heat level, interiority is the engine: the gap between what a
  character does and what they want is where the genre lives.

## Heat level

Declare it in NOVEL.md (closed door / warm / explicit) and keep it consistent;
heat-level whiplash breaks reader trust faster than almost anything. Scenes of
intimacy follow the same delivery rules as action scenes: they must change the
relationship state or they are filler.

## Trope ledger

| Trope | Watch for |
|---|---|
| Enemies to lovers | The enmity must have real teeth early or the turn means nothing |
| Fake relationship | The pretense must force real intimacy, scene by scene |
| Second chance | Requires an honest account of why it failed the first time |
| Grumpy/sunshine | The grump's warmth must be earned and specific, not switched on |
| Love triangle | Resolves on character change, not on one rival's demotion |

## Failure modes (review flags)

- **The misunderstanding plot:** the dark moment could be solved by one honest
  sentence. The genre's most-cited reader complaint.
- **Chemistry by assertion:** the narration insists on attraction the scenes never
  demonstrate.
- **Interchangeable love interest:** the lead could be swapped for anyone of the
  same description; their specific wound and want never shape events.
- **External plot eats the book:** the suspense/fantasy plot advances while the
  relationship state stalls for chapters.
- **Unearned repair:** a grand gesture without demonstrated change underneath it.
